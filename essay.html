Black Friday, The Super Bowl, and American Consumerism

     In the United States, two annual events stand out for their cultural significance and widespread appeal: The Super Bowl and Black Friday. The holiday shopping season kicks off on Black Friday, the day after Thanksgiving where promotional sales and big discounts are the order of the day. This is the day consumers go in droves into stores or online in search of the best deals; the busiest shopping day in the year. The Super Bowl, the NFL championship game, is also more than merely a sporting event; it is a cultural event. Millions of viewers watch the game not only for the game itself but also for the halftime performances and the highly anticipated commercials.

     While these events are distinct in nature—one rooted in retail and the other in sports—they both highlight aspects of American consumer culture. Black Friday has become synonymous with the consumerist frenzy, often criticized for encouraging excessive spending and disregard for the environmental impact of mass consumption. The Super Bowl, beyond being a sports spectacle, has evolved into a showcase for consumerism through its extravagant advertisements and the significant consumption associated with Super Bowl parties and merchandise. Black Friday and the Super Bowl reflect and shape American consumer habits, highlighting the environmental and societal impacts of intense shopping and entertainment culture.

     Black Friday, once a busy shopping day following Thanksgiving, has transformed into a global symbol of rampant consumerism. This transformation is not merely a cultural shift but also an environmental concern. According to the National Retail Federation, almost 70% of Americans are expected to shop during the weekend that stretches from Black Friday to Cyber Monday. The day's activities, heavily promoted through advertising, contribute significantly to a larger carbon footprint. Throughout the following days after Black Friday, vans and trucks are moving en masse to get orders delivered, as “81% of online Black Friday purchases include a home delivery service rather than collecting in person” as stated by Staveley Head. This massive movement of goods, primarily through delivery services, intensifies the environmental impact. The surge in deliveries, predominantly by diesel-powered vehicles, leads to a spike in carbon emissions and air pollution. Moreover, the packaging waste generated from these millions of deliveries adds to the already significant environmental burden, contributing to increased landfill waste and resource depletion. This chain of events underscores the profound ecological footprint of Black Friday, extending far beyond the point of sale.

     The environmental impact of Black Friday goes beyond the immediate carbon emissions from increased shopping activity. It's part of a broader 'work and spend' cycle, as identified in this article by The Ecologist. This cycle, fueled by advertising, leads to more hours worked, higher earnings, and consequently, increased consumption. This pattern of behavior has significant ecological implications. For example, the scale effect, also described in the article, indicates that more work hours lead to more consumption, which, when scaled up, results in high levels of economic activity and associated environmental damage. Additionally, the composition effect suggests that longer work hours reduce the time available for sustainable activities, like using eco-friendly transportation or engaging in low-impact hobbies.

     Research on the Super Bowl's environmental impact reveals significant air quality concerns, particularly in increased levels of Particulate Matter (PM10) on game day. Data from the EPA’s Air Quality System shows an increase in pollution across the area surrounding the stadium, with a notable spike in pollution levels at the stadium itself. This increase in harmful particulates not only poses health risks but also reflects the broader environmental costs of hosting such large-scale events. This data only focuses on the effects from the live event itself, not even taking into account all the resources used for gatherings outside of the stadium. For instance, over 1 billion chicken wings, the staple food of the Super Bowl, are consumed on the day of the event. The combined effect of televisions, heating or cooling systems, and other appliances at home-parties contributes to a significant increase in energy consumption, adding to the event's overall ecological footprint.

     In terms of consumerism, the Super Bowl stands as a monumental event in American culture, driving a surge in consumer spending and advertising. It's not just a sports event; it's a marketing juggernaut. Companies spend millions on commercials, capitalizing on the game's vast audience to promote products and services. This advertising blitz contributes to a culture of consumption, where the allure of products and deals often overshadows the game itself. The Super Bowl, therefore, is more than an athletic competition; it's a reflection of American consumer habits, where entertainment and consumption intertwine, shaping public perception and spending behaviors.

     Moving from the case of Black Friday and the Super Bowl which exemplify the greater American consumerism trends. This pattern entails high levels of consumption and spending, which are well embedded in the cultural and economical fabric of the nation. However, consumerism is not just buying products; it is an American lifestyle that has been carefully developed for decades, fueled by advertising and the promise to be happy or popular through things. In the long run, this uninterrupted quest for goods and services has far-reaching economic, social and environmental repercussions.

     This consumer culture contributes to one of the key environmental effects, the pressure on natural resources. The generation, consumption and disposal of goods uses lots of materials and energy, causing depletion of resources. For example, the location of trash dumps is an obvious symptom of this. Landfill sites increase, and they occupy precious land space, causing contamination of soil and water among other environmental problems, as consumer goods are rapidly produced, used and thrown away. Consumerism in fact affects natural resources in many more ways that go beyond the landfills. Resource extraction comes as a result of demand for new products from mining for metals to deforestation for paper and wood products. Often, this process of extraction leads to the degradation of habitat, loss of biodiversity, and contributes to climate change. Apart from it, a lot of energy is spent in the production and transport of these raw materials which again is contributing to global warming.

     In essence, American consumerism, while a driver of economic growth and a symbol of lifestyle aspirations, comes with a hefty environmental price tag. The depletion of natural resources, the expansion of landfill sites, and the associated environmental degradation are critical issues that need addressing. This calls for a shift towards more sustainable consumption practices and a reevaluation of the values that underpin consumer culture in America.
