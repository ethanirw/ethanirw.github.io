Black Friday, The Superbowl, and American Consumerism

Scroll to continue

In the U.S., Black Friday and the Super Bowl are major cultural events. Black Friday kicks off the holiday shopping season with widespread sales, drawing crowds nationwide. The Super Bowl goes beyond sports, captivating millions with its halftime shows and commercials. Despite their joyous nature, both events exemplify American consumer culture: Black Friday with its shopping frenzy and the Super Bowl with its lavish ads and party consumption. They not only mirror but also influence American consumer habits, impacting both society and the environment.

Black Friday has become more than just a busy shopping day; it's a symbol of extreme consumerism with serious environmental impacts. The day's heavy advertising leads to a surge in online shopping, resulting in a flood of deliveries. This not only increases air pollution and carbon emissions from transport but also adds to packaging waste, affecting landfills and resource use. The statistics, such as the overwhelming percentage of Americans participating and the high volume of home deliveries, underline the scale of these environmental issues. Furthermore, Black Friday feeds into a cycle of working more to spend more, which sidelines sustainable practices. This reflects a consumer culture that often prioritizes buying over environmental health, influencing long-term lifestyle choices and posing challenges to sustainability.

The Super Bowl has a significant impact on both the environment and consumerism. Air pollution ( measured in PM10) spikes around the stadium on game day, highlighting the environmental costs of such large events. This extends to home parties, where energy consumption rises due to increased use of TVs and appliances. Consumerism-wise, the Super Bowl is a major cultural event, driving substantial advertising and spending. Companies invest heavily in commercials, promoting a culture of consumption that often eclipses the game itself. The Super Bowl thus reflects American consumer habits, merging entertainment with consumption and shaping public spending and perceptions.

Conclusion

American consumerism, evident in events like Black Friday and the Super Bowl, drives high consumption and spending, deeply impacting the environment. This lifestyle leads to significant resource depletion and environmental degradation. The cycle of producing, consuming, and discarding goods expands landfills and strains natural resources. Manufacturing consumer products demands intensive resource use, contributing to habitat destruction, biodiversity loss, and increased carbon emissions. These issues highlight the urgent need for sustainable consumption practices and a reevaluation of consumer culture in America to reduce its environmental footprint.
